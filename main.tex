\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Merit-based scholarship programs are often viewed as regressive
because eligibility correlates with family income. Using tax-linked
data from \textcite{chetty2020mobility}, we show that Tennessee's
HOPE Scholarship instead generates a \emph{progressive compositional
shift} at public colleges: the share of students from the bottom
income quintile rises while the top-quintile share falls, with all
five quintiles shifting monotonically. Total enrollment remains
unchanged, indicating that the effect operates through sorting
across institutional sectors rather than expansion of college
attendance.

We formalize this mechanism in a three-destination sorting model
and decompose the effect into a permanent price channel (the
scholarship's price reduction) and a cyclical income-sensitivity
channel (responsiveness to local economic shocks). Merging
county-level income data, we show that tuition-scaling programs
buffer these shocks---producing a second monotonic quintile gradient
estimated on independent variation---while fixed-dollar programs
produce a third gradient of opposite sign at four-year colleges, as
marginal students created by the scholarship downshift to two-year
institutions during downturns. Three five-for-five monotonic
gradients, each from independent identifying variation, converge on
the same price-theoretic mechanism. Cross-state evidence supports the
model: programs with moderate eligibility thresholds and large
public-sector subsidies generate progressive sorting, while
high-threshold programs generate regressive composition changes.
Program design determines both the direction and durability of merit
aid's distributional effects.
\end{abstract}
