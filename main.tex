\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Merit-based scholarship programs are widely viewed as regressive
because eligibility correlates with family income. Using tax-linked
data from \textcite{chetty2020mobility}, we show that Tennessee's HOPE
Scholarship produces a \emph{progressive} compositional shift at
public colleges: all five income quintiles shift monotonically, with
bottom-quintile share rising and top-quintile share falling. Total
enrollment is unchanged---the shift reflects three-destination sorting
across public, private, and out-of-state alternatives. We decompose
this effect into a permanent substitution effect (the relative price
reduction from the scholarship) and a cyclical income effect
(sensitivity to local economic downturns). Merging BEA county income
data, we find that tuition-scaling programs (Florida, West Virginia)
compensate the income effect---composition is stable during
downturns---while fixed-dollar programs (Tennessee, Georgia) do not,
as marginal students reallocate across tiers. This produces a second
five-for-five monotonic quintile gradient, estimated on entirely
different variation, consistent with the adoption design. NLSY97
microdata confirm that the eligibility gradient across income
quintiles is steep and monotonic, consistent with the model's core
prediction. Results survive Rambachan--Roth sensitivity at
$\Delta_\text{RM} \leq 1$ and $\Delta_\text{SD} \leq 0.005$ under
the preferred specification with economic controls. A permutation
test over balanced-panel states ranks Tennessee as the most extreme
for both outcomes ($p = 0.048$). Program design---threshold location,
award structure, and Pell interaction---jointly determines the
direction and durability of compositional change across states.
\end{abstract}
