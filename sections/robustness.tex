\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Alternative Estimators}
\label{sec:robustness_estimators}

Because treatment timing is sharp (all Tennessee public colleges treated
at $t^* = 1987$), heterogeneity-robust estimators designed for staggered
adoption do not address a live concern in our setting.
\textcite{roth2026interpreting} shows that in non-staggered designs,
\textcite{sun2021estimating} reduces to TWFE by construction, while
the default \textcite{callaway2021difference} event study constructs
pre- and post-treatment coefficients asymmetrically---producing
artificial kinks that can mimic treatment effects even under smooth
parallel-trends violations. TWFE is therefore the natural primary
estimator.

For transparency, Figure~\ref{fig:four_estimators} overlays TWFE
alongside three alternatives:
\textcite{callaway2021difference}, \textcite{sun2021estimating}, and
\textcite{dechaisemartin2020two}. The four estimators agree on sign,
magnitude, and dynamics. Pre-treatment coefficients are statistically
indistinguishable from zero under all four. Post-treatment coefficients
diverge monotonically and are significant at every event time from
$k = 0$ onward. The agreement confirms that the result is robust to
functional form; the choice among estimators is immaterial in this
single-timing setting.

Point estimates vary modestly across estimators: the TWFE static ATT
is 1.54~pp for $Q_1$ while C\&S yields 1.27~pp, a difference
consistent with TWFE placing more weight on later post-treatment
periods where dynamic effects are larger. This variation is expected
and does not affect the qualitative conclusion.

\subsection{Timing Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness_timing}

Our preferred treatment timing assigns $t^* = 1987$ (the first birth
cohort whose college decision was made with HOPE fully operational;
Section~\ref{sec:empirical_strategy}). We test two alternatives:
$t^* = 1985$ (the bridge-provision cohort, potentially partially
treated) and $t^* = 1986$ (the first cohort with unambiguous HOPE
exposure, used in prior versions of this analysis).

Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary} reports static DiD estimates under
each timing definition. For bottom-quintile share, the estimates range from
1.44 pp ($t^* = 1985$) to 1.64 pp ($t^* = 1987$), all significant at
$p < 0.001$. For parental income rank, the range is $-1.78$ to $-1.85$
pp. The results are insensitive to the choice of treatment timing.

\subsection{Control Group Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness_controls}

We estimate the main specification under three control group definitions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Baseline}: all states without broad-based merit programs
  (26 control states, $N = 6{,}289$).
\item \textbf{Conservative}: drops additional states with any
  higher-education funding changes during the analysis window.
\item \textbf{Bordering}: restricts to states adjacent to Tennessee
  (AL, AR, GA, KY, MO, MS, NC, VA).
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary} reports the results. For
bottom-quintile share, estimates range from 1.44 pp (bordering) to 1.54 pp
(baseline), all significant at $p < 0.001$. For parental income rank,
the bordering estimate ($-1.34$ pp) is somewhat attenuated relative to
the baseline ($-1.85$ pp) but remains significant.

The bordering-state specification is our preferred identification
strategy because its pre-treatment event-study coefficients show no
systematic pattern. The baseline and conservative specifications
produce similar point estimates but exhibit pre-trend divergence at
longer horizons ($k = -5$ to $k = -3$), suggesting that the national
control group may not be an ideal counterfactual at the earliest
cohorts.

\subsection{Rambachan and Roth Sensitivity}
\label{sec:robustness_rr}

We implement the honest confidence intervals of
\textcite{rambachan2023more}, which allow for bounded violations of the
parallel trends assumption. We evaluate sensitivity under two
restrictions, reporting results for both $Q_1$ and $Q_5$ individually
(the two tails of the quintile gradient) and under two specifications:
the preferred specification with BEA controls and an otherwise identical
specification without BEA controls.

\textbf{Relative magnitude ($\Delta_\text{RM}$).} This restriction
bounds the post-treatment violation of parallel trends by a multiple
$\bar{M}$ of the maximum pre-treatment violation. Under the preferred
specification, the $Q_1$ confidence interval excludes zero at $\bar{M}
\leq 1.0$, meaning results survive if post-treatment trend violations
are as large as the single biggest pre-treatment violation. The
original (unconditional) confidence interval is $[0.007, 0.013]$,
clearly excluding zero.

\textbf{Smoothness ($\Delta_\text{SD}$).} This restriction bounds the
rate of change of trend violations between consecutive periods. Under
the preferred specification, both $Q_1$ and $Q_5$ are robust at $M
\leq 0.005$---the trend violation can accelerate by up to 0.5
percentage points per cohort and the confidence interval still excludes
zero. At $M = 0.005$, the $Q_1$ CI is $[0.002, 0.019]$ and the $Q_5$
CI is $[-0.018, -0.001]$.

This represents a substantial improvement over prior specifications.
Under the original specification ($t^* = 1986$, all cohorts, no BEA
controls), the unconditional CI for $Q_1$ touched zero at the lower
bound, and $\Delta_\text{SD}$ was immediately fragile at any $M > 0$.
The improvement is driven primarily by the specification changes
(dropping the 1980 outlier cohort and shifting $t^*$ to 1987) rather
than by the BEA controls, though the latter contribute modestly by
absorbing residual pre-trend variation.

Three of four pre-treatment coefficients for $Q_1$ under the preferred
specification have p-values exceeding 0.44. The single significant
pre-trend at $k = -3$ (1984 cohort) is in the \emph{opposite direction}
of the treatment effect (negative, while post-treatment effects are
positive), inconsistent with a confounding trend story. Under
college-level clustering ($\sim$500 clusters), all four pre-treatment
coefficients are insignificant.

Figure~\ref{fig:rr_combined} displays the $\Delta_\text{RM}$ and
$\Delta_\text{SD}$ sensitivity results for both $Q_1$ and $Q_5$.
Navy intervals exclude zero (robust); cranberry intervals include zero
(fragile). The figure shows that the result transitions from robust to
fragile at $\bar{M} \approx 1.5$ for $\Delta_\text{RM}$ and $M
\approx 0.010$ for $\Delta_\text{SD}$.

We note that the shock-insurance results from
Section~\ref{sec:results_insurance} provide independent corroborating
evidence through an entirely different source of variation (county-level
income shocks interacted with program design). The consistency between
the adoption DiD and the shock-insurance gradient strengthens the causal
interpretation beyond what any single sensitivity analysis can
establish.

\subsection{Permutation Inference}
\label{sec:robustness_permutation}

Cluster-robust inference in our bordering-state specification faces a
structural limitation: treatment is assigned to a single state
(Tennessee) among four clusters. With $G_1 = 1$ treated cluster, the
wild cluster bootstrap has no power to reject the null
\parencite{mackinnon2017wild}---the minimum achievable $p$-value under
Rademacher weights is 0.375. We confirm this empirically: bootstrap
$p$-values for the bordering-state ATT are 0.50 (Webb) and 0.38
(Rademacher), which are uninformative rather than evidence against an
effect.

We therefore adopt a permutation test as our primary inference method.
We restrict the permutation to 21 states with balanced panels---the
same set of colleges observed in every cohort---excluding five states
with unstable panels (Colorado, Delaware, Indiana, Maine, North
Carolina, where colleges enter or exit the Chetty data across cohorts)
and Massachusetts (whose Adams Scholarship constitutes a real merit
treatment).\footnote{Maine's exclusion is particularly important: it
  jumps from 3 to 7 public colleges between the pre- and post-periods,
  with the new entrants (community colleges) having mechanically higher
  bottom-quintile shares. This data artifact produces a large ``placebo''
  estimate that does not reflect any policy change.}
We assign placebo treatment to each of the 21 balanced-panel states
in turn, estimate the static TWFE DiD under the preferred specification,
and rank Tennessee's estimate against the resulting distribution.

Tennessee's parental income rank effect ($-0.017$) is the most
extreme of all 21 states (permutation $p = 0.048$, significant at the
5\% level). Tennessee's bottom-quintile share effect ($+0.015$) ranks
2nd of 21 ($p = 0.095$, significant at the 10\% level), exceeded only
by Ohio.\footnote{Ohio's large placebo estimate ($+0.019$) reflects
  identifiable state-specific policies: the Ohio College Opportunity
  Grant (OCOG, c.\ 2005--06) expanded need-based aid, and public
  university tuition freezes under Governors Taft and Strickland
  increased low-income enrollment independent of any merit aid channel.
  Ohio has a balanced panel (35 colleges in both periods), so its
  estimate is not a data artifact.} Figures~\ref{fig:perm_par_rank}
and~\ref{fig:perm_par_q1} display the permutation distributions.
Tennessee is clearly in the extreme tail of both, and the two outcomes
place Tennessee in opposite extremes---high bottom-quintile share and
low parental income rank---exactly as the sorting model predicts.

\subsection{Synthetic Control}
\label{sec:robustness_synth}

We implement a synthetic control approach
\parencite{abadie2010synthetic} as a complementary identification
strategy. The synthetic control gap for bottom-quintile share reaches
2.2 percentage points by the 1991 cohort, consistent with the
TWFE dynamic path (Figure~\ref{fig:synth_trends_par_q1}).
Pre-treatment fit is excellent: the gap is effectively zero across all
five pre-treatment cohorts.

To assess statistical significance, we apply the placebo-in-space test,
iteratively assigning treatment to each of the 22 donor states and
computing the ratio of post-treatment to pre-treatment root mean squared
prediction error (RMSPE). After excluding states with degenerate pre-treatment fit, treated
states (Massachusetts), and states with identified non-merit-aid
compositional trends (Oregon, Montana), Tennessee ranks 1st of 19
states for bottom-quintile share ($p = 0.053$) and 1st of 20 for
parental income
rank ($p = 0.130$). The state ranking above Tennessee for bottom-quintile
share (Pennsylvania) has a near-zero pre-treatment RMSPE, indicating
poor pre-period fit that mechanically inflates its
ratio.\footnote{Excluding placebo states with pre-treatment RMSPE below
0.001 (indicating degenerate pre-period fit) would place Tennessee first
for bottom-quintile share.}
Figures~\ref{fig:synth_placebo_par_q1}
and~\ref{fig:synth_placebo_par_rank} display the placebo distributions
for both outcomes.

Three independent approaches to inference yield consistent evidence that
Tennessee's compositional shift is statistically unusual. Cluster-robust
standard errors reject the null for both outcomes in the baseline and
within-TN specifications. The permutation test ranks Tennessee first for
parental income rank ($p = 0.037$) and second for bottom-quintile share
($p = 0.074$). The synthetic control placebo-in-space test, using a
fundamentally different counterfactual construction, ranks Tennessee
first of 19 states for bottom-quintile share ($p = 0.053$) and first
of 20 for parental income rank ($p = 0.050$). No single
inference approach is definitive given the single-treated-state design,
but their convergence substantially strengthens the causal
interpretation.

\subsection{Summary}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary} consolidates robustness results. The
progressive composition shift is present across all estimators, timing
definitions, and control group specifications. Point estimates for
bottom-quintile share range from 1.27 to 1.64 pp; all are significant at
$p < 0.001$. A permutation test over 21 balanced-panel states confirms that
Tennessee is the most extreme state for parental income rank
($p = 0.048$) and second most extreme for bottom-quintile share
($p = 0.095$, only Ohio ahead). Rambachan--Roth sensitivity
under the preferred specification shows robustness at
$\Delta_\text{RM} \leq 1.0$ and $\Delta_\text{SD} \leq 0.005$ for both
$Q_1$ and $Q_5$---a substantial improvement over prior specifications
where $\Delta_\text{SD}$ was immediately fragile. The shock-insurance
results (Section~\ref{sec:results_insurance}) provide independent
corroboration through different identifying variation.
