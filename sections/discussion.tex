\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Georgia: A Regressive Prediction Confirmed}

Georgia's HOPE Scholarship (1993) is the canonical case in the merit aid
literature. \textcite{dynarski2000hope} documents that Georgia HOPE
widened the attendance gap between higher- and lower-income students,
with Black students---disproportionately Pell-eligible---showing no
attendance gain. Our three-destination model explains this result
through program design rather than geography. From 1993 to 2000, Georgia
HOPE awards were reduced dollar-for-dollar by Pell Grant amounts,
eliminating the access margin for the lowest-income families (see
Section~\ref{sec:theory_georgia}). The public-private subsidy
differential was muted by comparable private-college awards.
\textcite{cornwell2006enrollment} confirm that four-year private
enrollment in Georgia \emph{rose} by 13.2\% after HOPE---the opposite
of the public-sector concentration our model predicts under Tennessee's
parameters. Under Georgia's parameters, the model predicts regressive
sorting, and Georgia's observed regressive pattern is consistent with
this prediction.

\subsection{West Virginia: Enrollment Contraction Confounds Composition}

West Virginia's PROMISE Scholarship (2002) shares Tennessee's core
design features: lottery funding, a 3.0 GPA threshold, no Pell offset,
and subsidies concentrated at public institutions
\parencite{scottclayton2011money}. If program design alone determined
the direction of compositional sorting, WV should replicate Tennessee's
progressive result. It does not. The TWFE ATT for bottom-quintile
share is $-1.21$ pp ($p < 0.001$), indicating a regressive shift.

The explanation is straightforward: total enrollment at WV public
colleges declined by 155 students per cohort ($p < 0.001$) during the
analysis period, driven by demographic contraction rather than
compositional sorting. Bottom-quintile count fell by 26 per cohort
($p < 0.001$). The modest TWFE share increase for Q1 is a denominator
artifact of this enrollment decline, not progressive sorting.
Balanced-panel estimates restricted to WV colleges with complete data
yield null results. West Virginia is uninformative as a test of the
sorting mechanism---the institutional context prevents the
three-destination mechanism from operating---rather than a contradiction
of the Tennessee finding.

\subsection{The Decomposition Interpretation}
\label{sec:discussion_decomposition}

The adoption DiD and the shock-interaction analysis are two empirical
manifestations of a single theoretical framework. The adoption DiD
captures the \emph{substitution effect} of introducing a merit
scholarship: the permanent relative price reduction that generates
predictions P1--P7. The shock-interaction analysis captures the
\emph{income effect} of economic fluctuations: the cyclical sensitivity
of composition to local affordability shocks, which the scholarship
may or may not compensate (predictions P8--P11).

The Tennessee finding illustrates both components. HOPE generates a
progressive compositional shift on average (substitution effect:
$+1.3$ pp for $Q_1$) but does not stabilize that shift during economic
downturns (income effect: zero aggregate buffering, $p = 0.97$).
\textcite{bruce2014jackpot} document the mechanism: HOPE creates
marginal four-year students---low-income families who attend four-year
colleges only because the scholarship makes it affordable---who
downshift to two-year colleges when family income falls. The
scholarship keeps them in the system but does not prevent tier
reallocation (Proposition~\ref{prop:tier}).

Florida Bright Futures, by contrast, generates both. The Medallion
tier's percentage-based award (75\% of tuition) produces progressive
sorting and compensates the income effect: the five-for-five insurance
gradient shows near-complete neutralization of income shocks across
the entire quintile distribution. West Virginia's PROMISE (full tuition
coverage) similarly buffers. The distinguishing feature is not
threshold height or program generosity in dollars, but whether the
award \emph{scales with costs}---the theoretical distinction formalized
in Proposition~\ref{prop:compensation}.

This interpretation reconciles an otherwise puzzling pattern. Tennessee
and Georgia---the two states with the highest per-student merit-aid
expenditures---show the weakest shock buffering. Florida and West
Virginia---with lower per-student expenditures---show the strongest.
Raw generosity does not determine resilience; award structure does.

\subsection{Mobility Implications}

Descriptive evidence suggests that the progressive composition shift
did not come at the cost of diminished economic returns.
Figure~\ref{fig:tn_mobility_trends} shows that upward mobility
rates at Tennessee public colleges tracked below the national control
average in the pre-HOPE period and converged toward it afterward.
Despite substantial access expansion, upward mobility did not
decline.\footnote{We explored an instrumental variables strategy
using a shift-share (Bartik) instrument, but pre-trend tests reveal
that the instrument predicts mobility outcomes in the pre-treatment
period (joint $F = 20.6$, $p < 0.001$), indicating an exclusion
restriction violation. The conservative reading from the descriptive
evidence is that colleges whose bottom-quintile representation rose
did not experience worse average outcomes.}

\subsection{Gender Heterogeneity and the Eligibility Channel}
\label{sec:gender_mechanism}

The three-destination model predicts that compositional sorting operates
through the price channel: the scholarship $S$ reduces the effective
cost of public college for students whose ability exceeds the
eligibility threshold $\tau$. We exploit a sharp implication of this
mechanism. Tennessee HOPE requires a 3.0 high school GPA. Female
students clear this threshold at substantially higher rates than male
students---a well-documented consequence of the persistent gender gap
in high school academic performance
\parencite{goldin2006homecoming}.\footnote{Nationally, the female
  high school GPA distribution is shifted rightward by approximately
  0.2--0.3 grade points. Among 2004 high school graduates,
  approximately 40\% of female students held GPAs above 3.0, compared
  to 30\% of male students \parencite{nces2023digest}.} If HOPE's
composition effect operates through the eligibility-price channel,
the progressive shift should be larger for the gender with a higher
eligibility rate.

We test this prediction using gender-specific parental income measures
from \textcite{chetty2020mobility}. We pool Tennessee and Massachusetts
as treated states (staggered at $t^* = 1987$ and $t^* = 1988$
respectively) against all remaining states and estimate a stacked
interaction model with college-by-gender fixed effects,
cohort-by-gender fixed effects, and state-specific linear gender
trends.\footnote{State-specific linear gender trends absorb
pre-existing differential drift in male versus female composition
across states, which is present in the raw data and produces
misleading pre-trend violations without detrending. After detrending,
the pre-treatment gender interaction is statistically
indistinguishable from zero ($p = 0.913$).}

The female-specific progressive shift in bottom-quintile share is
0.96~pp larger than the male shift (SE $= 0.20$~pp, $p < 0.001$).
To interpret: the overall HOPE effect on $Q_1$ share is approximately
$+1.5$~pp. The gender decomposition reveals that this average masks
substantial heterogeneity---the female-specific effect is roughly
$+2.0$~pp while the male-specific effect is roughly $+1.0$~pp. Women
benefit nearly twice as much as men, consistent with their higher
eligibility rate (40\% vs.\ 30\% clearing the 3.0 GPA threshold).

The gender differential is larger in the bottom-quintile share than
in the mean parental income rank. The par\_rank gender gap is
$-0.44$~pp (SE $= 0.14$~pp, $p = 0.002$), statistically significant
but substantively modest relative to the $Q_1$ share gap. Both
outcomes move in the predicted direction---women shift the composition
of public colleges more than men---but the effect concentrates at the
access margin. Low-income women newly eligible for HOPE face the
sharpest change in their effective choice set: without HOPE, many
would not attend a four-year public institution; with HOPE, the price
drops substantially. Higher-income students attend college regardless
of HOPE, so the gender-eligibility mechanism has less traction in the
middle and upper quintiles. The resulting composition shift is largest
in the tail of the income distribution---more visible in share
measures than in the mean.

This test has a key identification advantage over the main DiD
specification. Male and female students attend the same colleges in the
same birth cohorts. Any college-level confound---changes in local
economic conditions, institutional quality, or admissions
policy---affects both genders equally and is absorbed by the
college-by-gender fixed effects. Any cohort-level confound---federal
financial aid changes, national labor market shifts---is similarly
absorbed. For a secular trend to produce the observed gender gap, it
would need to differentially shift the income composition of female
versus male students at Tennessee public colleges relative to control
states, in a pattern that begins precisely at the HOPE treatment date.
No plausible alternative satisfies this requirement.
Figure~\ref{fig:gender_event} displays the gender-specific event
studies. Both series track near zero in the pre-period and diverge after
treatment onset, with the female series rising more steeply.

The gender channel also connects to the tier reallocation mechanism
documented in Section~\ref{sec:results_tier_reallocation}. The
shock-interaction analysis finds that the four-year anti-buffering
effect is substantially larger for female students ($\hat\beta_2 =
-0.128$, $p = 0.005$) than for male students ($-0.054$, $p = 0.151$).
This is the dynamic counterpart of the static gender result: women
are more likely to qualify for HOPE, more likely to be the marginal
four-year students the scholarship creates, and therefore more
sensitive to income shocks at the four-year/two-year margin. The
scholarship enables their upward tier substitution in good times and
exposes them to downward reallocation in bad times---precisely the
income-effect channel formalized in
Proposition~\ref{prop:tier}.

The gender result provides our most direct test of the
theoretical mechanism. The three-destination model's price channel
predicts a specific pattern---larger progressive shifts for the gender
with higher eligibility rates---and the data confirm it with high
statistical precision. This evidence complements the cross-state
comparisons in the following subsections, which test the model's
predictions along the program-design dimension rather than the
eligibility dimension.

\subsection{Massachusetts Adams Scholarship}

The Adams Scholarship (2004) provides tuition waivers at Massachusetts
public colleges for students scoring in the top 25\% of their district
on the MCAS exam \parencite{cohodes2014merit}. Like Tennessee's HOPE,
the subsidy is concentrated at public institutions---private colleges
receive no benefit---creating a public-private price differential. The
eligibility threshold (top 25\% of district) is moderate, qualifying a
broad share of the income distribution.

The TWFE ATT for bottom-quintile share at Massachusetts public colleges
is $+1.38$ percentage points ($p < 0.001$), remarkably close to
Tennessee's $+1.42$ pp. Mean parental income rank declines by $1.08$
percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The Massachusetts result
provides independent validation of the Tennessee finding: a second
state with a moderate eligibility threshold and a public-sector subsidy
concentration produces a progressive compositional shift of similar
magnitude.

\subsection{South Dakota Opportunity Scholarship}

South Dakota's Opportunity Scholarship requires a minimum ACT score of
24 (approximately the 75th percentile nationally) and provides a
comparatively small award (\$1,000--\$5,000 per year). The high
threshold and small subsidy satisfy the model's conditions for
regressive sorting: only upper-income students are likely to qualify,
and the price differential is too small to induce meaningful sector
switching.

The TWFE ATT confirms this prediction: bottom-quintile share at South
Dakota public colleges \emph{declines} by $1.47$
percentage points ($p < 0.001$), and mean parental income rank
\emph{increases} by $3.25$ percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The
effect is strongly regressive---the opposite of Tennessee and
Massachusetts. The quintile decomposition is also inverted: Q1 through
Q3 lose share, Q4 and Q5 gain share.

\subsection{Staggered DiD: Pooled Multi-State Evidence}

We formalize the cross-state evidence using a staggered
Callaway--Sant'Anna design that pools multiple treatment states with
different adoption dates against never-treated controls. The preferred
pooled specification combines Tennessee ($t^* = 1987$) and
Massachusetts ($t^* = 1987$), which share similar design
parameters (moderate threshold, public-sector subsidy concentration)
and produce individually similar progressive effects. The pooled ATT
for bottom-quintile share is $+0.012$ ($p < 0.001$), nearly identical
to the Tennessee-only estimate---confirming that the progressive
composition effect replicates independently.

A broader staggered design including all five strong-merit states with
pre-treatment periods---Nevada ($t^* = 1983$), West Virginia
($t^* = 1985$), Tennessee, Massachusetts, and South Dakota (both
$t^* = 1987$)---produces a near-zero overall ATT ($+0.005$,
$p = 0.28$). This reflects heterogeneous group-specific effects that
cancel in aggregation: the TN/MA/SD group produces a progressive
effect ($+0.011$, $p < 0.001$), while Nevada ($-0.005$, $p = 0.012$)
and West Virginia ($-0.010$, $p < 0.001$) are regressive. The zero
overall effect is evidence \emph{for} the model, not against it: the
staggered design correctly separates programs with different design
parameters into groups with opposite-signed effects, exactly as the
theory predicts.

Nevada's regressive result is consistent with its eligibility profile:
NLSY97 data show that only 13\% of bottom-quintile students qualify
under its 3.25~GPA threshold
(Section~\ref{sec:data_eligibility}), placing it in the restrictive
tier alongside South Dakota and Florida Academic Scholars.

\subsection{Generalizability and Policy Implications}

The cross-state evidence extends beyond a single treated state.
Massachusetts independently produces a progressive shift of similar
magnitude under similar program parameters, while South Dakota's
regressive shift confirms the model's prediction under high thresholds.
The shock-insurance analysis adds Florida and West Virginia as new
testable cases---states whose programs predate the Chetty panel and
were previously untestable through adoption DiD.

The three-destination model, extended with the Slutsky decomposition,
organizes all cases through a common set of design parameters:
eligibility threshold (determines \emph{who} is eligible), public-private
subsidy differential (determines \emph{where} students sort),
Pell interaction (determines \emph{whether} low-income students benefit),
and award structure (determines \emph{whether} progressive sorting
persists during economic fluctuations). No single parameter is
sufficient; the combination determines both the direction and
durability of compositional change.

For policymakers designing merit-aid programs to promote low-income
access, the findings suggest four principles. First, the eligibility
threshold must be low enough that substantial numbers of low-income
students qualify (moderate GPA, OR rather than AND conditions).
Second, the subsidy must be concentrated at public institutions to
create a meaningful price differential. Third, the program should not
offset existing need-based aid (no Pell displacement). Fourth---and
this is the new contribution of the shock-insurance analysis---the
award should scale with tuition costs (percentage-based or full-tuition
coverage) rather than provide a fixed dollar amount. Fixed-dollar
awards generate progressive sorting but leave it vulnerable to
economic downturns; tuition-scaling awards generate progressive sorting
\emph{and} sustain it.

The WV non-replication highlights an additional scope condition:
program design is necessary but not sufficient. Enrollment contraction
at WV public colleges overwhelmed compositional dynamics in the
adoption DiD, though WV PROMISE shows the strongest shock-buffering
effect among all programs. A stable or growing public system with
absorptive capacity is required for the sorting mechanism to operate.
