\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Georgia: A Regressive Prediction Confirmed}

Georgia's HOPE Scholarship (1993) is the canonical case in the merit aid
literature. \textcite{dynarski2000hope} documents that Georgia HOPE
widened the attendance gap between higher- and lower-income students,
with Black students---disproportionately Pell-eligible---showing no
attendance gain. Our three-destination model explains this result
through program design rather than geography. From 1993 to 2000, Georgia
HOPE awards were reduced dollar-for-dollar by Pell Grant amounts,
eliminating the access margin for the lowest-income families (see
Section~\ref{sec:theory_georgia}). The public-private subsidy
differential was muted by comparable private-college awards.
\textcite{cornwell2006enrollment} confirm that four-year private
enrollment in Georgia \emph{rose} by 13.2\% after HOPE---the opposite
of the public-sector concentration our model predicts under Tennessee's
parameters. Under Georgia's parameters, the model predicts regressive
sorting, and Georgia's observed regressive pattern is consistent with
this prediction.

\subsection{West Virginia: Enrollment Contraction Confounds Composition}

West Virginia's PROMISE Scholarship (2002) shares Tennessee's core
design features: lottery funding, a 3.0 GPA threshold, no Pell offset,
and subsidies concentrated at public institutions
\parencite{scottclayton2011money}. If program design alone determined
the direction of compositional sorting, WV should replicate Tennessee's
progressive result. The evidence is ambiguous. The TWFE static
estimate for bottom-quintile share is $+0.55$ pp ($p = 0.041$),
superficially progressive. But the Callaway--Sant'Anna group-specific
ATT reverses the sign to $-0.99$ pp ($p < 0.001$), indicating a
regressive shift. The two estimators disagree because WV's pre-trends
are unstable, and the event-study coefficients show no coherent
post-treatment pattern.

The underlying problem is enrollment contraction. Total enrollment at
WV public colleges declined by 155 students per cohort ($p < 0.001$)
during the analysis period, driven by demographic contraction rather
than compositional sorting. Bottom-quintile count fell by 26 per
cohort ($p < 0.001$). The TWFE's modestly positive share estimate
is a denominator artifact of this enrollment decline, not progressive
sorting. Balanced-panel estimates restricted to WV colleges with
complete data yield null results. West Virginia is uninformative as a
test of the sorting mechanism---enrollment contraction overwhelms the
compositional signal---rather than a contradiction of the Tennessee
finding.

\subsection{Massachusetts Adams Scholarship}

The Adams Scholarship (2004) provides tuition waivers at Massachusetts
public colleges for students scoring in the top 25\% of their district
on the MCAS exam \parencite{cohodes2014merit}. Like Tennessee's HOPE,
the subsidy is concentrated at public institutions---private colleges
receive no benefit---creating a public-private price differential. The
eligibility threshold (top 25\% of district) is moderate, qualifying a
broad share of the income distribution.

The TWFE ATT for bottom-quintile share at Massachusetts public colleges
is $+1.38$ percentage points ($p < 0.001$), remarkably close to
Tennessee's $+1.42$ pp. Mean parental income rank declines by $1.08$
percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The Massachusetts result
provides independent validation of the Tennessee finding: a second
state with a moderate eligibility threshold and a public-sector subsidy
concentration produces a progressive compositional shift of similar
magnitude.

\subsection{South Dakota Opportunity Scholarship}

South Dakota's Opportunity Scholarship requires a minimum ACT score of
24 (approximately the 75th percentile nationally) and provides a
comparatively small award (\$1,000--\$5,000 per year). The high
threshold and small subsidy satisfy the model's conditions for
regressive sorting: only upper-income students are likely to qualify,
and the price differential is too small to induce meaningful sector
switching.

The TWFE ATT confirms this prediction: bottom-quintile share at South
Dakota public colleges \emph{declines} by $1.47$
percentage points ($p < 0.001$), and mean parental income rank
\emph{increases} by $3.25$ percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The
effect is strongly regressive---the opposite of Tennessee and
Massachusetts. The quintile decomposition is also inverted: Q1 through
Q3 lose share, Q4 and Q5 gain share.

\subsection{Four-State Comparison}

The preceding subsections analyze each state individually. We now
compare all four states with estimable adoption DiDs---Tennessee,
Massachusetts, South Dakota, and West Virginia---selected because
they span the model's predicted range from progressive to regressive
sorting and have sufficient pre-treatment periods in the Chetty panel.

Figure~\ref{fig:four_state} displays event-study estimates for
bottom-quintile share across all four states. The visual contrast is
striking: Tennessee and Massachusetts trend upward after treatment
(progressive), South Dakota trends sharply downward (regressive), and
West Virginia shows no clear pattern (null, confounded by enrollment
contraction).

\subsection{The Decomposition Interpretation}
\label{sec:discussion_decomposition}

The adoption DiD and the shock-interaction analysis are two empirical
manifestations of a single theoretical framework. The adoption DiD
captures the \emph{price effect} of introducing a merit
scholarship: the permanent relative price reduction that generates
predictions P1--P7. The shock-interaction analysis captures the
\emph{income-sensitivity effect} of economic fluctuations: the
cyclical sensitivity of composition to local affordability shocks,
which the scholarship may or may not compensate (predictions
P8--P11).

The Tennessee finding illustrates both components. HOPE generates a
progressive compositional shift on average (price effect:
$+1.4$ pp for $Q_1$) but does not stabilize that shift during economic
downturns (income-sensitivity effect: zero aggregate buffering,
$p = 0.97$).
\textcite{bruce2014jackpot} document the mechanism: HOPE creates
marginal four-year students---low-income families who attend four-year
colleges only because the scholarship makes it affordable---who
downshift to two-year colleges when family income falls. The
scholarship keeps them in the system but does not prevent tier
reallocation (Proposition~\ref{prop:tier}).

Florida Bright Futures, by contrast, generates both. The Medallion
tier's percentage-based award (75\% of tuition) produces progressive
sorting and compensates the income-sensitivity effect: the
five-for-five insurance
gradient shows near-complete neutralization of income shocks across
the entire quintile distribution. West Virginia's PROMISE (full tuition
coverage) similarly buffers. The distinguishing feature is not
threshold height or program generosity in dollars, but whether the
award \emph{scales with costs}---the theoretical distinction formalized
in Proposition~\ref{prop:compensation}.

This interpretation reconciles an otherwise puzzling pattern. Tennessee
and Georgia---the two states with the highest per-student merit-aid
expenditures---show the weakest shock buffering. Florida and West
Virginia---with lower per-student expenditures---show the strongest.
Raw generosity does not determine resilience; award structure does.

\subsection{Gender Heterogeneity and the Eligibility Channel}
\label{sec:gender_mechanism}

The three-destination model predicts that compositional sorting
operates through the price channel: the scholarship $S$ reduces the
effective cost of public college for students whose ability exceeds
the eligibility threshold $\tau$. The composition effect at each
point in the income distribution is therefore the product of two
forces: the eligibility rate (the fraction of students who clear
$\tau$) and price sensitivity (how much the subsidy changes the
enrollment decision). If eligibility rates vary systematically across
observable subgroups, the model generates testable predictions about
heterogeneous treatment effects---predictions that are difficult to
reconcile with alternative explanations.

We exploit the gender gap in high school GPA to construct such a
test. Tennessee HOPE requires a 3.0 GPA. Female students clear this
threshold at substantially higher rates than male students---a
well-documented consequence of the persistent gender gap in high
school academic performance \parencite{goldin2006homecoming}. Using
nationally representative NLSY97 transcript data, we estimate
gender-specific eligibility rates at each income quintile
(Figure~\ref{fig:eligibility_gender}). The gender gap in eligibility
is large and statistically significant at every quintile: at $Q_1$,
40\% of women but only 22\% of men exceed 3.0 ($p < 0.001$); at
$Q_5$, the rates are 68\% and 49\% ($p < 0.001$). The gap is
approximately 16--19 percentage points across the entire income
distribution.\footnote{The NLSY97 is a nationally representative
  sample, not Tennessee-specific. The eligibility rates reflect the
  pre-existing gender-GPA gradient---a structural feature of high
  school academic performance that is not caused by any particular
  state's merit aid program. Using national data is conservative: it
  avoids the concern that Tennessee's GPA distribution was endogenous
  to HOPE.}

The model's prediction follows directly: because more women are
eligible at every income level, the effective price reduction is
larger for women, and the female composition shift should exceed the
male shift. We test this using gender-specific parental income
quintile shares from \textcite{chetty2020mobility}, Table~5.

\paragraph{Aggregate gender interaction.}

We estimate a TWFE interaction model on Tennessee public colleges
against non-merit national controls, with college-by-gender fixed
effects, cohort-by-gender fixed effects, and state-specific linear
gender trends.\footnote{State-specific linear gender trends absorb
  pre-existing differential drift in male versus female composition
  across states, which is present in the raw data. After detrending,
  the pre-treatment gender interaction is statistically
  indistinguishable from zero. Pooling Tennessee and Massachusetts
  yields a qualitatively similar but attenuated gender gap ($+0.96$
  pp, $p < 0.001$), consistent with the mechanism operating through
  the GPA threshold specifically---Massachusetts uses a different
  eligibility criterion (top 25\% MCAS score), which dilutes the
  GPA-specific gender channel.}

The female-specific progressive shift in bottom-quintile share is
$+1.23$~pp larger than the male shift (SE $= 0.23$~pp,
$p < 0.001$). The base male effect is $+1.22$~pp ($p = 0.001$), so
the female effect is approximately $+2.45$~pp---women gain twice as
much representation at the bottom of the income distribution as men.
For mean parental income rank, the gender gap is $-0.34$~pp
(SE $= 0.16$~pp, $p = 0.039$): both outcomes move in the predicted
direction, but the effect concentrates in the bottom-quintile
share---the access margin where the eligibility channel has the most
traction.

\paragraph{Quintile decomposition by gender.}

The aggregate interaction establishes that women gain more. The
quintile decomposition reveals \emph{where} in the income distribution
each gender's effect concentrates, and the answer differs in a way
the model predicts.

Figure~\ref{fig:gender_quintile_gradient} displays the quintile
gradient separately for female and male students. The female gradient
is steeply monotonic: $Q_1$ gains $+2.3$~pp ($p < 0.001$), $Q_2$
through $Q_3$ are near zero, and $Q_5$ loses $-2.0$~pp
($p < 0.001$). The male gradient has a qualitatively different shape:
$Q_1$ gains $+1.2$~pp ($p = 0.001$), but $Q_2$ gains \emph{more}
than $Q_1$ at $+1.4$~pp ($p < 0.001$), before declining through
$Q_4$ and $Q_5$.

The eligibility rates in Figure~\ref{fig:eligibility_gender} explain
this contrast. The composition effect at each quintile is the product
of the eligibility rate and price sensitivity. For women, eligibility
at $Q_1$ is already high (40\%), so price sensitivity---which is
largest at the bottom of the income distribution---dominates. The
result is a steep monotonic gradient driven by the access margin:
low-income women who clear the GPA threshold face the sharpest change
in their effective choice set.

For men, $Q_1$ eligibility is low (22\%). The few $Q_1$ men who
qualify produce a positive but attenuated effect. At $Q_2$, male
eligibility is similar (23\%), but $Q_2$ students sit at a different
margin: they are more likely to have been considering college
already, and the scholarship tips the public-versus-private or
in-state-versus-out-of-state decision rather than the
attend-versus-not-attend decision. The product of eligibility and
price sensitivity peaks at $Q_2$ for men, producing the non-monotonic
hump. From $Q_3$ onward, price sensitivity declines and the male
gradient turns negative.

The non-monotonic male gradient is difficult to explain without
reference to the eligibility threshold. A generic
Tennessee-specific shock---fiscal, demographic, or
institutional---would shift male and female composition in the same
direction and with the same shape. A story that operates through
the GPA threshold predicts exactly what we observe: a monotonic
female gradient (high eligibility lets price sensitivity dominate)
and a non-monotonic male gradient (low $Q_1$ eligibility suppresses
the access margin and shifts the peak to $Q_2$).

\paragraph{Identification.}

This test has a key advantage over the main DiD specification. Male
and female students attend the same colleges in the same birth
cohorts. Any college-level confound---changes in local economic
conditions, institutional quality, or admissions policy---affects
both genders equally and is absorbed by the college-by-gender fixed
effects. Any cohort-level confound is similarly absorbed. For a
secular trend to produce the observed gender gap, it would need to
differentially shift the income composition of female versus male
students at Tennessee public colleges relative to control states, in
a pattern that begins precisely at the HOPE treatment date and
produces gradient shapes that match the gender-specific eligibility
profiles. No plausible alternative satisfies this requirement.
Figure~\ref{fig:gender_event} displays the gender-specific event
studies. Both series track near zero in the pre-period and diverge
after treatment onset, with the female series rising more steeply
for bottom-quintile share and declining more steeply for mean
parental income rank.

The gender channel also connects to the tier reallocation mechanism
documented in Section~\ref{sec:results_tier_reallocation}. The
shock-interaction analysis finds that the four-year anti-buffering
effect is substantially larger for female students ($\hat\beta_2 =
-0.128$, $p = 0.005$) than for male students ($-0.054$,
$p = 0.151$). Women are more likely to qualify for HOPE, more likely
to be the marginal four-year students the scholarship creates, and
therefore more sensitive to income shocks at the four-year/two-year
margin---the dynamic counterpart of the static gender result.

\subsection{Policy Interpretation and Scope Conditions}

The cross-state evidence is consistent with the model's predictions
beyond a single treated state. Massachusetts independently produces
a progressive shift of similar magnitude under similar program
parameters, while South Dakota's regressive shift is consistent with
the model's prediction under high thresholds.
The shock-insurance analysis adds Florida and West Virginia as new
testable cases---states whose programs predate the Chetty panel and
were previously untestable through adoption DiD.

The three-destination model, extended with the price/income-sensitivity
decomposition,
organizes all cases through a common set of design parameters:
eligibility threshold (determines \emph{who} is eligible), public-private
subsidy differential (determines \emph{where} students sort),
Pell interaction (determines \emph{whether} low-income students benefit),
and award structure (determines \emph{whether} progressive sorting
persists during economic fluctuations). No single parameter is
sufficient; the combination determines both the direction and
durability of compositional change.

For policymakers, the cross-state patterns are consistent with four
design principles, though no single comparison isolates each parameter
cleanly. First, the eligibility threshold should be low enough that
substantial numbers of low-income students qualify (moderate GPA, OR
rather than AND conditions). Second, the subsidy should be concentrated
at public institutions to create a meaningful price differential.
Third, the program should not offset existing need-based aid (no Pell
displacement). Fourth---and this is the new contribution of the
shock-insurance analysis---the award should scale with tuition costs
(percentage-based or full-tuition coverage) rather than provide a
fixed dollar amount. Fixed-dollar
awards generate progressive sorting but leave it vulnerable to
economic downturns; tuition-scaling awards generate progressive sorting
\emph{and} sustain it.

The WV non-replication highlights an additional scope condition:
program design is necessary but not sufficient. Enrollment contraction
at WV public colleges overwhelmed compositional dynamics in the
adoption DiD, though WV PROMISE shows the strongest shock-buffering
effect among all programs. A stable or growing public system with
absorptive capacity is required for the sorting mechanism to operate.
