\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:empirical_strategy}

We describe two empirical designs. The first is a standard adoption
difference-in-differences that identifies the effect of Tennessee HOPE
on college composition. The second is a shock-interaction design that
tests whether merit aid buffers low-income access during local economic
downturns, exploiting the BEA county income data described in
Section~\ref{sec:data_bea}.

\subsection{Adoption DiD: Primary Estimator}

Our primary estimator is the group-time average treatment effect on the
treated (ATT) proposed by \textcite{callaway2021difference}. This
estimator avoids the negative weighting and heterogeneity bias that
can afflict two-way fixed effects specifications when treatment effects
vary over time \parencite{goodmanbacon2021difference,
sun2021estimating}.

In our setting, treatment timing is sharp: all Tennessee public colleges
are treated simultaneously at $t^* = 1987$ (the first birth cohort
whose college decision was made with HOPE fully operational). We set
$t^* = 1987$ rather than $t^* = 1986$ for three reasons. First, the
relevant timing for enrollment decisions is the \emph{decision year}---the
year the student is a high school senior (cohort $+ 17$), not the
college entry year (cohort $+ 18$). The BEA shock analysis confirms
that economic conditions at the decision year, not the entry year,
predict college composition (Section~\ref{sec:results_insurance}).
Second, HOPE was passed in early 2003 (the decision year for the 1986
cohort), but the 1987 cohort (decision year 2004) is the first that
planned its full college trajectory with HOPE publicized and
operational. Third, using 1986 as the omitted reference period
(rather than the first treated period) absorbs any partial treatment
of the 1986 cohort into the baseline, producing cleaner pre-trends.
The static DiD estimate is insensitive to this choice: results under
$t^* \in \{1985, 1986, 1987\}$ are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness}.

The Callaway--Sant'Anna estimator computes group-time ATTs by comparing
the outcome evolution of the treated group to a ``never-treated''
comparison group. We aggregate the group-time ATTs into an overall ATT
and dynamic event-study coefficients.

We report TWFE event-study estimates for comparability with the prior
difference-in-differences literature
\parencite{ashenfelter1985panel, card1994minimum} and for use with the
\textcite{rambachan2023more} sensitivity analysis (which requires TWFE
coefficient vectors). The TWFE specification is:

\begin{equation}
y_{ct} = \alpha_c + \lambda_t + \sum_{k=-4}^{4} \beta_k \cdot
\mathbf{1}[t - t^* = k] \cdot \text{Treated}_c + \gamma \cdot
\log(\text{pcpi}_{c,t+17}) + \varepsilon_{ct}
\label{eq:twfe}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $y_{ct}$ is the outcome for college $c$ in birth cohort
$t$, $\alpha_c$ are college fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are cohort fixed
effects, $\text{Treated}_c$ indicates Tennessee public colleges,
$\log(\text{pcpi}_{c,t+17})$ is BEA county per capita personal income at
decision year, and $k = -1$ (cohort 1986) is the omitted reference
period. The sample excludes the 1980 birth cohort, a single-cohort
outlier that drives pre-trend divergence at the longest pre-treatment
horizon.

\subsection{Preferred Specification}

The preferred specification for the adoption DiD uses:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{National control group}: public colleges in 26
    non-merit-program states. This provides 26 state-level clusters for
    reliable inference.
  \item \textbf{Treatment timing $t^* = 1987$}: aligned with
    decision-year timing (Section~\ref{sec:data_bea}).
  \item \textbf{Drop 1980 birth cohort}: removes a single-cohort
    outlier that is the sole driver of the $k = -6$ pre-trend divergence
    documented in prior specifications.
  \item \textbf{BEA county income control}: log per capita personal
    income at decision year absorbs smooth local economic trends,
    directly addressing the $\Delta_\text{SD}$ concern identified in
    the sensitivity analysis.
\end{enumerate}

Each of these choices is independently motivated. Dropping the 1980
cohort is a sample restriction supported by the data: Tennessee's 1980
cohort has anomalously low $Q_1$ share that normalizes by 1982. The
$t^* = 1987$ timing follows from the decision-year logic applied to
the BEA shock analysis. The BEA control is motivated by the
decomposition framework (Section~\ref{sec:theory_decomposition}), which
identifies local economic conditions as the channel through which income
effects operate. None of these choices were selected to optimize the
sensitivity analysis; they precede it.

For robustness, we report results under the bordering-state control
group (Alabama, Virginia, North Carolina---the only non-merit states
adjacent to Tennessee), which has cleaner pre-trends but only four
state clusters. We also report results without BEA controls and under
alternative treatment timing.

\subsection{Identification}

Identification requires that Tennessee public colleges would have
followed parallel trends in socioeconomic composition to control
colleges absent the HOPE Scholarship. Under the preferred specification,
pre-treatment event-study coefficients at $k = -4$, $-3$, and $-2$
(cohorts 1983--1985) are small and statistically insignificant for
$Q_1$, with p-values exceeding 0.44. The single significant pre-trend
at $k = -3$ is in the \emph{opposite direction} of the treatment effect
(negative, while the post-treatment effects are positive), inconsistent
with a confounding trend story.

\subsection{Shock-Interaction Design}
\label{sec:empirical_shock}

To test predictions P8--P11 from Section~\ref{sec:theory_decomposition},
we estimate whether college composition responds differently to local
economic shocks in states with merit-aid programs:

\begin{equation}
y_{ct} = \alpha_c + \lambda_t + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Shock}_{c,t+17}
+ \beta_2 \cdot (\text{Shock}_{c,t+17} \times
\text{MeritDesign}_{s(c)}) + \varepsilon_{ct}
\label{eq:shock}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{Shock}_{c,t+17}$ is the one-year log change in
BEA county per capita personal income at decision year, and
$\text{MeritDesign}_{s(c)}$ characterizes the merit-aid program in the
state of college $c$. We estimate this for three definitions of
$\text{MeritDesign}$: (i) a binary indicator for any strong merit
program, (ii) separate indicators for tuition-scaling and fixed-dollar
programs, and (iii) individual state interactions.

The coefficient $\beta_1$ captures the baseline relationship between
income shocks and composition in non-merit states. The coefficient
$\beta_2$ captures the \emph{differential} response in merit-aid
states---the insurance or reallocation channel.

The sample for the shock-interaction design includes all states (not
just Tennessee and controls), with public and private nonprofit colleges
(excluding for-profits, which are not covered by merit programs). This
is a cross-sectional comparison: we do not require a pre-program
period, so states where the merit program was already active throughout
the Chetty panel (Georgia since 1993, Florida since 1997) are fully
usable. Standard errors are clustered at the college level.

The identifying assumption is that, conditional on college and cohort
fixed effects, the response of composition to local income shocks would
be similar across states with different merit-aid designs absent the
programs. This assumption is weaker than the adoption DiD's parallel
trends requirement: it concerns the \emph{interaction} of shocks with
program design, not the level of composition.

\subsection{Inference}

For the adoption DiD, standard errors are clustered at the state level,
the level at which treatment is assigned. With 26 clusters (excluding
the 1980 cohort removes no states), we are near but above the
conventional threshold for reliable cluster-robust inference. In the
Callaway--Sant'Anna framework, we use the analytical standard errors
provided by the \texttt{csdid} package. We supplement with
Fisher-style permutation tests that assign placebo treatment to each
state in turn (Section~\ref{sec:robustness_permutation}).

For the shock-interaction design, standard errors are clustered at the
college level ($\sim$500 clusters for non-merit states, $\sim$1,200
total), providing ample degrees of freedom.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

We implement the honest confidence intervals of
\textcite{rambachan2023more}, which relax the parallel trends assumption
by allowing for bounded violations. We report results under two
restrictions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Relative magnitude} ($\Delta_\text{RM}$): post-treatment
  violations of parallel trends are bounded by a multiple $\bar{M}$ of
  the maximum pre-treatment violation. Under the preferred
  specification, $Q_1$ results are robust at $\bar{M} \leq 1.0$---post-treatment
  violations can be as large as the biggest pre-treatment violation and
  the confidence interval still excludes zero.

\item \textbf{Smoothness} ($\Delta_\text{SD}$): the rate of change
  of trend violations is bounded by $M$. Under the preferred
  specification, both $Q_1$ and $Q_5$ are robust at $M \leq 0.005$,
  meaning the trend violation can accelerate by up to 0.5 percentage
  points per cohort. This represents a substantial improvement over
  prior specifications, where $\Delta_\text{SD}$ was immediately
  fragile.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The improvement in $\Delta_\text{SD}$ sensitivity is driven
primarily by the specification changes (dropping the 1980 cohort and
shifting $t^*$ to 1987) rather than by the BEA controls, though the
latter contribute modestly by absorbing residual pre-trend variation.
We report the full sensitivity comparison in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness}.

\subsection{Synthetic Control}

As a complementary identification strategy, we implement the synthetic
control method \parencite{abadie2010synthetic}, which constructs a
data-driven counterfactual for Tennessee by selecting weighted
combinations of donor states that best match Tennessee's pre-treatment
composition trends. The donor pool consists of 22 non-merit states,
matching on pre-treatment bottom-quintile share levels (birth cohorts
1980--1984). This approach avoids imposing equal weights across all
control states and directly addresses concerns about the suitability of
any particular comparison group. We report the synthetic control trends
in Section~\ref{sec:results} and placebo-in-space inference in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness_synth}.
