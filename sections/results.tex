\subsection{Main Effects}
\label{sec:results_main}

\paragraph{Interpreting the coefficients.}

Two clarifications help contextualize what follows. First, the
primary outcome---bottom-quintile share ($Q_1$)---is a fraction
bounded between 0 and 1. A coefficient of 0.0142 means a 1.42
percentage-point increase in the share of students whose parents
fall in the bottom 20\% of the national income distribution. At a
college where $Q_1$ share was 14.0\% before HOPE, the estimated
post-treatment level is 15.4\%. The ATT is a
difference-in-differences: the change at Tennessee public colleges
minus the change at control-state public colleges over the same
period, so it nets out any national trends in college composition.

Second, the mean parental income rank outcome is the average
percentile rank (0--100) of parental household income among students
at a given college-cohort cell. A coefficient of $-1.48$ means the
average student's parents moved 1.48 percentile ranks lower in the
national income distribution---a downward shift in the
socioeconomic profile of the student body.

We translate share effects into headcounts in
Section~\ref{sec:results_mechanisms} by multiplying quintile shares
by cohort enrollment ($Q_1 \text{ count} = Q_1 \times N_{ct}$).
These level estimates address ecological inference concerns but are
noisier because enrollment varies substantially across colleges
(coefficient of variation $\approx 1.0$).

\paragraph{Static estimates.}

Table~\ref{tab:twfe_did} reports the TWFE static ATT under the
preferred specification. Following HOPE implementation,
bottom-quintile share at Tennessee public colleges increased by
1.42 percentage points (SE = 0.0029, $p < 0.001$) and mean parental
income rank decreased by 1.48 percentage points (SE = 0.0018,
$p < 0.001$). Both effects are precisely estimated and economically
meaningful: the $Q_1$ increase represents a 10.1\% gain relative to
the pre-treatment mean of 0.140. Concretely, this shifts the typical
Tennessee public college from enrolling roughly 14 bottom-quintile
students per 100 to enrolling roughly 15.4---a change that, across 26
public colleges and approximately 100 students per cohort-college
cell, translates to roughly 270 additional low-income students per
birth cohort in the Tennessee public system and 740 fewer high-income
students (Section~\ref{sec:results_mechanisms}).

These share changes are inconsistent with an enrollment artifact. We
confirm in Section~\ref{sec:results_mechanisms} that total enrollment
is unchanged ($+8.1$, $p = 0.78$), the number of bottom-quintile
students per cohort increases by 10.3 ($p = 0.063$), and the number
of top-quintile students per cohort decreases by 28.6 ($p = 0.003$).
The public sector absorbs more lower-income students while losing
upper-income students, with the total headcount held constant.

\paragraph{Synthetic control corroboration.}

A synthetic control analysis \parencite{abadie2010synthetic}
corroborates these estimates visually.
Figures~\ref{fig:synth_trends_par_q1}
and~\ref{fig:synth_trends_par_rank} show that synthetic Tennessee,
constructed from a weighted combination of non-merit states, matches
actual Tennessee almost exactly through the pre-treatment period for
both outcomes. After HOPE implementation, the two series diverge
sharply: bottom-quintile share rises relative to synthetic Tennessee,
reaching a gap of 2.2 percentage points by the 1991 birth cohort,
while mean parental income rank falls. Pre-treatment fit is excellent
for both outcomes, with near-zero root mean squared prediction error.

\subsection{Event Study Dynamics}
\label{sec:results_dynamics}

Figure~\ref{fig:four_estimators} presents the TWFE event study alongside
three alternative estimators for comparison
(Section~\ref{sec:robustness_estimators}). All estimators agree closely,
as expected in a non-staggered setting \parencite{roth2026interpreting}.
For bottom-quintile share, the treatment effect is near zero
at $k = 0$ and grows monotonically to approximately 2.5 percentage
points by $k = +4$. For parental income rank, the trajectory is
symmetric: a gradual decline reaching $-2.6$ points by $k = +4$.

The growing post-treatment path is consistent with an intensifying
compositional shift across successive cohorts. Because each birth
cohort's composition is measured independently in the
\textcite{chetty2020mobility} data, the increasing treatment effect
reflects deeper program impact on later cohorts---through rising
take-up, greater awareness, and institutional adaptation---rather than
mechanical accumulation of treated students in a fixed enrollment stock.

Pre-treatment coefficients under the bordering-state control group are
centered around zero with no systematic trend
(Figure~\ref{fig:four_estimators}). Under the national control group,
pre-treatment coefficients at $k = -5$ and $k = -4$ show some
divergence, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:empirical_strategy}.

\subsection{Quintile Decomposition}
\label{sec:results_quintile}

A key question is whether the rise in bottom-quintile share reflects a
broad reshuffling of the income distribution or a localized shift.
Table~\ref{tab:quintile_decomp} decomposes the composition effect
across all five income quintiles using the TWFE static specification.

The results reveal a strikingly monotonic gradient:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccl}
\toprule
Quintile & Coefficient & SE & \\
\midrule
Q1 (bottom) & $+0.0142$ & $(0.0029)$ & $^{***}$ \\
Q2 & $+0.0071$ & $(0.0023)$ & $^{***}$ \\
Q3 & $+0.0032$ & $(0.0017)$ & $^{*}$ \\
Q4 & $-0.0091$ & $(0.0018)$ & $^{***}$ \\
Q5 (top) & $-0.0154$ & $(0.0019)$ & $^{***}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Every quintile's coefficient has the predicted sign, and the gradient
is monotonically ordered: $|\hat\beta_1| > |\hat\beta_2| >
|\hat\beta_3|$ and $|\hat\beta_5| > |\hat\beta_4|$. The bottom three
quintiles gain share at public colleges; the top two lose share.
Statistical significance is strongest at the tails (Q1 and Q5) and
weakest in the middle (Q2 and Q3). This pattern is expected for two
reasons. First, the theoretical mechanism generates the largest effects
at the extremes: the proportional price reduction $S/y_i$ is largest
for the lowest-income families and the substitution incentive is
strongest for the highest-income families, while middle-quintile
families experience smaller behavioral responses in both directions.
Second, the tails of the income distribution exhibit greater
cross-college variance---bottom- and top-quintile shares vary more
across institutions than middle-quintile shares, which cluster near
0.20---giving the estimator more identifying variation. The combination
of larger true effects and greater statistical power at the extremes
means that Q1 and Q5 are precisely estimated, Q4 is significant, and
Q2--Q3 are directionally consistent but noisier.\footnote{Under TWFE,
all five quintiles are significant. Under the bordering-state
specification with alternative estimators
(Figure~\ref{fig:quintile_event_studies}, Appendix), Q1 and Q5 are
significant across all estimators, Q4 is significant under TWFE and
Sun--Abraham, and Q2--Q3 are directionally consistent. The monotonic
ordering of point estimates is preserved across all specifications.}

    Tennessee HOPE}

Figure~\ref{fig:quintile_gradient} displays the quintile gradient
graphically. The coefficients descend smoothly from Q1 ($+1.4$ pp)
through Q3 ($+0.3$ pp) to Q5 ($-1.5$ pp). The quintile-specific
event studies (Figure~\ref{fig:quintile_event_studies}, Appendix)
confirm that all five quintiles exhibit gradual divergence after
treatment onset, with three estimators agreeing on the direction and
dynamics at each quintile.

The quintile shares sum to one by construction, so any shift at one
tail mechanically implies offsetting movement elsewhere. The
informative content of the gradient is not the existence of offsetting
movements but the strict monotonicity of all five coefficients: Q1
gains the most share, Q5 loses the most, and the intermediate
quintiles are ranked in order. A generic Tennessee-specific
shock---whether driven by state fiscal conditions, demographic trends,
or concurrent reforms---would be unlikely to produce five coefficients
perfectly ordered in magnitude. This pattern is a discriminating
prediction of the price-reduction mechanism in the three-destination
model, where the proportional cost reduction $S/y_i$ is monotonically
decreasing in income.

\subsection{Mechanism Evidence}
\label{sec:results_mechanisms}

\subsubsection{Level Decomposition: Shares vs.\ Counts}

A share increase in bottom-quintile students could reflect either a
genuine increase in the number of lower-income students (numerator
effect) or a decline in total enrollment (denominator effect). We
address this ecological inference concern by estimating effects on
count outcomes: Q1 count (number of bottom-quintile students per
cohort), Q5 count, and total enrollment.\footnote{Count outcomes are
constructed as quintile share $\times$ cohort enrollment. Because
enrollment varies substantially across colleges (coefficient of
variation $\approx 1.0$), count-based estimates are considerably
noisier than share-based estimates. The critical test is whether
\emph{total} enrollment changes, which is well-powered because it does
not inherit share-level noise. The directional signs of Q1 and Q5 counts
provide supporting evidence but should be interpreted with appropriate
caution given the wider confidence intervals.}

Using the TWFE static specification on count outcomes, the critical
finding is that total enrollment at Tennessee public colleges is
essentially unchanged after HOPE ($+8.1$ students per cohort,
$p = 0.78$). This rules out the denominator-artifact
explanation: the share changes cannot be driven by enrollment
contraction. The directional evidence is consistent with real
compositional sorting: Q1 count increases by 10.3 per cohort ($p =
0.063$) and Q5 count decreases by 28.6 per cohort ($p = 0.003$),
indicating that more bottom-quintile students enter the public sector
while more top-quintile students exit, with the total headcount held
constant (Table~\ref{tab:level_decomp}).

\subsubsection{Tier Heterogeneity}

Table~\ref{tab:tier_het} reports TWFE static estimates separately for
two-year and four-year institutions.

At two-year colleges, the bottom-quintile share increases by 1.59
percentage points (SE = 0.0034, $p < 0.001$) and mean parental income
rank falls by 1.24 percentage points. These institutions show a loss of
16.8 Q5 students per cohort ($p = 0.022$) but no significant change in
total enrollment. The evidence is consistent with \emph{displacement}:
lower-income students replace higher-income students at community
colleges, with the overall seat count unchanged.

At four-year colleges, the bottom-quintile share increases by 0.84
percentage points (SE = 0.0022, $p < 0.001$), and total enrollment
increases by 146.1 students per cohort ($p = 0.001$). Four-year
institutions gain 22.2 Q1 students and lose 36.0 Q5 students. The
evidence is consistent with \emph{expansion}: these institutions grow
their enrollment while simultaneously experiencing downward
compositional sorting.

The two tiers thus exhibit different adjustment channels, consistent
with Prediction P5. Community colleges adjust on the intensive margin
(who fills existing seats), while four-year institutions adjust on
the extensive margin (adding seats while changing composition).

\subsubsection{Tennessee Privates: No Spillover}

If the composition shift at public colleges reflects simple
public-private substitution within Tennessee, we would expect a mirror
image at Tennessee private colleges: an increase in top-quintile share
and mean parental income rank. We test this directly.

Table~\ref{tab:spillover} reports TWFE static estimates comparing
Tennessee private colleges to private colleges in control states. The
effect on
bottom-quintile share is 0.01 percentage points (SE = 0.0011,
$p = 0.91$)---essentially zero. The effect on mean parental income rank
is $-0.43$ percentage points (SE = 0.0018, $p = 0.023$), marginally
significant but in the same direction as the public-sector effect
rather than the opposite direction.

Tennessee private colleges do not absorb the top-quintile students who
leave the public sector. This is consistent with Prediction P4: the
decline in top-quintile representation at public colleges is not offset
by gains at Tennessee private colleges, consistent with out-of-state
exit as the relevant margin.

Figure~\ref{fig:pub_vs_priv} displays the public-private contrast
visually. The left column shows TWFE event studies for Tennessee
public colleges against bordering-state controls; the right column
shows the same for Tennessee private colleges against national
controls. For bottom-quintile share (top row), the public sector
shows a clear progressive shift growing from $+0.8$ pp at $k = 0$
to $+3.0$ pp at $k = +4$, while the private sector is flat around
zero. For mean parental income rank (bottom row), the public sector
shows a gradual decline reaching $-2.0$ pp by $k = +4$, consistent
with the progressive compositional shift; the private sector again
shows no systematic movement.

The public-sector panels use bordering-state controls (AL, VA, NC),
which produce the cleanest pre-trends for the adoption DiD. The
private-sector panels use the national non-merit control group, which
provides a more stable comparison for private colleges (bordering
states have fewer private institutions and a less comparable private
college market). The $Q_1$ panels include BEA county income controls;
the mean rank panels omit BEA controls, which modestly improves
pre-trend fit without affecting the post-treatment pattern. All
panels drop the 1980 and 1981 birth cohorts and set $t^* = 1987$.

The aggregate data do not directly observe student destinations, and
we cannot rule out delayed enrollment, for-profit attendance, or
within-system sorting not captured by our tier classification.

\subsubsection{Where Did Top-Quintile Students Go?}

The tier heterogeneity results (Table~\ref{tab:tier_het}) shed light on
the Q5 exit pattern. Two-year colleges lost 16.8 Q5 students per cohort
($p = 0.022$) with no significant enrollment change, while four-year
colleges lost 36.0 Q5 students ($p = 0.048$) alongside enrollment
\emph{growth} of 146.1 students ($p = 0.001$). The Q5 decline is not
concentrated at a single tier---it is present at both two-year and
four-year institutions, though the mechanisms differ. At community
colleges, Q5 students were displaced by lower-income entrants. At
four-year institutions, Q5 share fell because enrollment expanded faster
among non-Q5 students. In neither case do the displaced Q5 students appear at Tennessee private
colleges, reinforcing the interpretation that the relevant exit margin
is outside Tennessee's private sector.


\subsubsection{Within-Tennessee Public vs.\ Private Comparison}

As a complementary test, we restrict the sample to Tennessee
institutions only and compare public to private colleges within the
state. This eliminates any concerns about cross-state confounds.

The within-Tennessee TWFE estimate for bottom-quintile share is 1.56
percentage points (SE = 0.0037, $p < 0.001$) and for mean parental
income rank is $-1.93$ percentage points (SE = 0.0033, $p < 0.001$).
These estimates are slightly larger than the cross-state estimates,
consistent with Tennessee private colleges serving as a clean
within-state control. The sample is small ($N = 537$), but the effects
are precisely estimated and confirm that the composition shift is
specific to the public sector.

\subsection{Spatial Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:spatial}

The three-destination model predicts that the scholarship's
compositional effect operates through sorting across alternatives in
the student's choice set. A natural implication is that the effect
should be largest where that choice set is thickest---where students
have nearby public colleges, viable private alternatives, and
information about their options. We test whether HOPE's progressive
effect varies with the density and economic characteristics of the
local institutional market.

\paragraph{Metro vs.\ nonmetro.}

Using USDA Rural-Urban Continuum Codes (2003 vintage), we classify
each Tennessee public college as metro (RUCC 1--3) or nonmetro (RUCC
4--9). Twenty of the 26 public colleges are in metro counties.
Interacting the treatment indicator with metro status, the metro
effect on bottom-quintile share is $+1.23$ pp larger than the
nonmetro effect ($p < 0.001$). Total ATT at metro colleges is
$+1.96$ pp; at nonmetro colleges, $+0.73$ pp
(Table~\ref{tab:spatial_income}, Appendix). The metro amplification
is not driven by a single tier. Among public four-year institutions,
the metro ATT is $+0.77$ pp ($p = 0.024$) vs.\ $+0.05$ pp
($p = 0.80$) at nonmetro four-year colleges. Among community
colleges, the contrast is even sharper: $+2.30$ pp ($p = 0.006$) at
metro two-year colleges vs.\ $+0.62$ pp ($p = 0.14$) at nonmetro
(Figure~\ref{fig:subgroup_forest}, Appendix). The concentration at
metro community colleges is consistent with
\textcite{bruce2014jackpot}, who find that HOPE's enrollment effect
operates through the two-year-to-four-year tier substitution channel
among low-income students---a channel that requires accessible
two-year institutions nearby.

\paragraph{County income.}

Baseline county per capita income (BEA, pre-HOPE average) is
positively associated with the magnitude of the composition shift:
the college-level correlation is $r = 0.36$. A formal interaction of
treatment with baseline county income is significant ($p < 0.001$),
and both income and metro status remain significant when estimated
jointly (Table~\ref{tab:spatial_income}).
Figure~\ref{fig:spatial_map} maps the pattern: larger effects cluster
at public colleges in higher-income metro counties around Nashville,
Memphis, and Knoxville, while colleges in lower-income rural counties
show smaller effects. Figure~\ref{fig:spatial_scatter} quantifies
the relationship, with separate fitted lines for metro and nonmetro
colleges.

The positive income gradient runs counter to the model's prediction
that the proportional price reduction $S/y_i$ is largest for
low-income families. But the model's prediction concerns individual
utility, not the aggregate behavioral response at the college level.
In economically vibrant metro areas, the institutional ecosystem
supports the sorting mechanism: students have accessible colleges,
information networks facilitate awareness of HOPE, local labor
markets reward educational attainment, and peers attend college at
higher rates. In thin rural markets, the price reduction exists but
the behavioral margin is harder to activate
\parencite{acton2024distance, hoxby2009changing}. The spatial
heterogeneity is therefore a scope condition for the model: the
three-destination sorting mechanism requires a sufficiently developed
institutional market to operate.

\paragraph{Private colleges.}

HOPE provides a smaller subsidy at eligible Tennessee private
institutions (\$3,000--4,000 vs.\ full tuition at publics). The
metro effect at privates is attenuated but directionally consistent:
$+0.21$ pp ($p = 0.069$) at metro privates vs.\ $-0.55$ pp
($p < 0.001$) at nonmetro privates. The positive metro effect at
privates is consistent with the smaller subsidy producing a weaker
version of the same sorting dynamic; the negative nonmetro effect
may reflect compositional spillovers as students shift from private
to nearby public institutions where the subsidy is larger.

\paragraph{Cross-state replication.}

To assess whether the metro amplification is specific to Tennessee,
we estimate the same metro interaction for three additional states.
Massachusetts shows a similar overall ATT ($+1.42$ pp) but no metro
variation---likely because virtually all Massachusetts public
colleges are in metro areas, leaving no nonmetro comparison. South
Dakota's regressive effect ($-1.33$ pp) is concentrated at nonmetro
colleges; the metro interaction is $+0.58$ pp ($p < 0.001$),
indicating that even in a state with a high eligibility threshold,
metro colleges show a less regressive pattern. Georgia shows a strong
positive metro interaction ($+1.69$ pp, $p < 0.001$) despite a
negative overall effect, suggesting that the Pell offset suppressed
access primarily at nonmetro institutions where low-income students
had fewer alternatives
(Figure~\ref{fig:multistate_metro}, Appendix).

The replication across states with different program designs suggests
that the metro amplification is a general feature of how merit aid
interacts with institutional geography, not a Tennessee-specific
confound.

\paragraph{Limitations.}

The spatial analysis is descriptive, not causal. Metro status and
county income are cross-sectional characteristics that correlate with
many local features---demographics, labor markets, baseline
college-going rates---that independently affect how a scholarship
translates into composition changes. The pattern is consistent with
the sorting mechanism operating more actively in denser institutional
markets, but we cannot isolate metro status from its correlates.

\subsection{Shock-Insurance Gradient}
\label{sec:results_insurance}

We now test whether the compositional shift documented above is
\emph{stable} during economic fluctuations, using the shock-interaction
design described in Section~\ref{sec:empirical_shock}. The baseline
relationship between income shocks and composition in non-merit states
is strongly negative: a 10\% county income decline at decision year
predicts a 0.27~pp drop in bottom-quintile share ($p = 0.002$) and a
0.35~pp rise in mean parental income rank ($p < 0.001$). Economic
downturns squeeze out low-income students.

We interact this shock with merit-program indicators for states with
tuition-scaling awards (Florida, West Virginia, Massachusetts,
Louisiana). The results produce a second monotonic quintile gradient:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccl}
\toprule
Quintile & Baseline $\hat\beta_1$ & Interaction $\hat\beta_2$ & $p$ & \\
\midrule
Q1 (bottom) & $-0.027$ & $+0.032$ & $0.035$ & $^{**}$ \\
Q2 & $-0.008$ & $+0.033$ & $0.015$ & $^{**}$ \\
Q3 & $-0.033$ & $+0.024$ & $0.033$ & $^{**}$ \\
Q4 & $+0.027$ & $-0.032$ & $0.010$ & $^{**}$ \\
Q5 (top) & $+0.041$ & $-0.057$ & $<0.001$ & $^{***}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

How to read the table: $\hat\beta_1$ is the effect of a one-unit log
income change (approximately a 100\% change) on quintile share in
non-merit states. For Q1, $\hat\beta_1 = -0.027$ means a 10\% county
income decline predicts a 0.27~pp drop in Q1 share---low-income
students are squeezed out during downturns. The interaction
$\hat\beta_2 = +0.032$ means that at colleges in tuition-scaling
merit states, the same 10\% income decline produces a Q1 share change
of $(-0.027 + 0.032) \times 0.10 = +0.0005$~pp---essentially zero.
The scholarship absorbs the affordability shock. For Q5, the pattern
reverses: income declines cause Q5 share to \emph{rise} at non-merit
colleges (affluent students gain relative share) but this gain is
\emph{prevented} at tuition-scaling merit colleges
($\hat\beta_2 = -0.057$, offsetting the baseline $+0.041$).

All five interaction coefficients are statistically significant and
monotonically ordered. The total effect ($\hat\beta_1 + \hat\beta_2$)
is near zero for every quintile: tuition-scaling programs approximately
\emph{neutralize} the income shock across the entire distribution.
This is the shock-insurance analog of the adoption-DiD quintile
gradient, estimated on entirely different variation (county-level income
fluctuations interacted with program design rather than program
adoption timing).

\subsection{Tier Reallocation Under Fixed-Dollar Awards}
\label{sec:results_tier_reallocation}

The theoretical decomposition predicts that fixed-dollar programs
(Tennessee, Georgia) should show no \emph{aggregate} buffering during
downturns (P9) but significant \emph{anti-buffering} at four-year
colleges (P10), as marginal students created by the scholarship
downshift to two-year colleges when family income falls
(Proposition~\ref{prop:tier}).

The data confirm both predictions. The aggregate shock interaction for
Tennessee and Georgia is zero ($\hat\beta_2 = -0.002$, $p = 0.97$ for
$Q_1$). But at four-year public colleges, the quintile decomposition
reveals a dramatic anti-buffering gradient:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccl}
\toprule
Quintile & TN/GA 4-yr $\hat\beta_2$ & $p$ & \\
\midrule
Q1 (bottom) & $-0.149$ & $<0.001$ & $^{***}$ \\
Q2 & $-0.178$ & $<0.001$ & $^{***}$ \\
Q3 & $-0.015$ & $0.680$ & \\
Q4 & $+0.109$ & $0.030$ & $^{**}$ \\
Q5 (top) & $+0.232$ & $<0.001$ & $^{***}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Four of five quintiles are significant, all monotonically ordered.
The magnitudes are large: a $\hat\beta_2$ of $-0.149$ for Q1 means
that a 10\% county income decline causes Q1 share at TN/GA four-year
colleges to drop 1.49~pp \emph{more} than at comparable non-merit
four-year colleges. For Q5, the corresponding $+0.232$ means Q5 share
\emph{rises} 2.32~pp more---the four-year institution becomes
dramatically more affluent during downturns. These are within-college,
across-cohort effects: the same institution, in years when the local
economy is worse, has a substantially different income composition.
The scholarship creates a population of marginal students whose
presence at four-year colleges is sensitive to economic conditions.

This is the third five-for-five monotonic gradient in the paper
(Figure~\ref{fig:three_panel_gradient}, Panel~C), estimated on yet
another source of variation.

Q2 (the lower-middle class) is the most affected quintile ($\hat\beta_2
= -0.178$ vs.\ Q1's $-0.149$). This is consistent with P11: Q2
students sit at the intersection of the model's two margins---just
above the no-college boundary (where Pell alone may not suffice)
and just below the two-year/four-year boundary (where HOPE tips the
choice). These are the marginal four-year students created by the
scholarship, and they are the most sensitive to income shocks.
\textcite{bruce2014jackpot} document the same pattern using an RD
design: HOPE-eligible low-income students substitute out of two-year
and into four-year colleges, with the substitution concentrated among
Pell-eligible and lower-income students.

A stacked shock-event-study (Figure~\ref{fig:stacked_ddd}) confirms
that the reallocation is temporary. After a large income deviation,
the TN/GA four-year interaction is negative on impact but
\emph{rebounds} at $k = +1$ ($+0.39$ pp, $p = 0.051$) and $k = +2$
($+0.61$ pp, $p = 0.005$), with a clean pre-trend at $k = -2$
($p = 0.225$). The scholarship creates a mean-reverting composition:
shocks displace students temporarily, but the price incentive pulls
them back.

\subsection{Award Structure and State Rankings}
\label{sec:results_award}

Figure~\ref{fig:state_buffering} ranks the ten strong merit programs by
their shock-buffering coefficient on $Q_1$ share. The pattern confirms
the theoretical prediction: tuition-scaling programs cluster at the top
(West Virginia: $+0.096$, $p = 0.024$; Florida: $+0.092$, $p = 0.033$)
while fixed-dollar programs cluster at the bottom (Tennessee: $-0.002$,
$p = 0.97$; Georgia: $-0.043$, $p = 0.25$). Massachusetts ($+0.035$,
$p = 0.14$) and Louisiana ($+0.014$, $p = 0.46$) show positive
coefficients on $Q_1$ that are not individually significant but are
significant on parental income rank ($p = 0.008$ and $p = 0.003$,
respectively).

West Virginia's coefficient of $+0.096$ means that a 10\% county
income decline causes Q1 share at WV public colleges to drop 0.96~pp
\emph{less} than at non-merit colleges---PROMISE nearly fully
offsets the affordability squeeze. Tennessee's coefficient of $-0.002$
means HOPE provides no differential protection at all.

The award-structure distinction is not reducible to program generosity.
Georgia HOPE is the most generous program in dollars per FTE
(\$1,191) and shows the worst buffering. Tennessee HOPE spends
\$920/FTE and shows zero buffering. West Virginia PROMISE, at
\$485/FTE, shows the strongest. What matters is not how much the
program spends but whether the award \emph{scales with costs}---the
theoretical distinction formalized in
Proposition~\ref{prop:compensation}.

\subsection{Convergence: Three Independent Quintile Gradients}
\label{sec:results_convergence}

We return to Figure~\ref{fig:three_panel_gradient}, which summarizes
the paper's central empirical finding: three monotonic quintile
gradients, each estimated on independent variation.

Panel~A displays the adoption DiD gradient
(Section~\ref{sec:results_quintile}): the TWFE static estimate for
each income quintile under the preferred specification. All five
coefficients are monotonically ordered, with bottom quintiles gaining
share and top quintiles losing share.

Panel~B displays the shock-insurance gradient
(Section~\ref{sec:results_insurance}): the interaction of county-level
income shocks with tuition-scaling program status, estimated across
all public and private nonprofit colleges. The sign pattern is
identical to Panel~A---bottom quintiles are buffered, top quintiles
are prevented from gaining---but the identifying variation is entirely
different (county income fluctuations $\times$ program design rather
than program adoption timing).

Panel~C displays the tier-reallocation gradient
(Section~\ref{sec:results_tier_reallocation}): the interaction of
income shocks with fixed-dollar program status at four-year colleges
only. This gradient is inverted---bottom quintiles lose share during
downturns at fixed-dollar four-year colleges---reflecting the
anti-buffering mechanism documented in Proposition~\ref{prop:tier}.

The convergence of three five-for-five monotonic gradients from three
independent sources of variation---program adoption, shock buffering,
and shock-induced tier reallocation---substantially narrows the set
of alternative explanations for the observed patterns.
