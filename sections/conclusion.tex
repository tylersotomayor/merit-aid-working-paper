\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We document that following Tennessee's HOPE Scholarship, public colleges
experienced a progressive shift in socioeconomic composition. The share of
bottom-quintile students increased by 1.3 percentage points, mean
parental income rank declined by 1.3 percentage points, and the effect is
monotonically ordered across all five income quintiles. Lower-income
students gained representation while higher-income students lost it.

This compositional change reflects sorting across sectors rather than
enrollment expansion. Total enrollment at Tennessee public colleges is
unchanged post-HOPE. The level decomposition confirms that the number
of bottom-quintile students increased while the number of top-quintile
students decreased. Top-quintile students who exit the public sector do
not appear at Tennessee private colleges, consistent with out-of-state
enrollment rather than in-state public-to-private substitution as the
relevant margin.

These results reconcile with the finding that merit aid widens aggregate
attendance gaps \parencite{dynarski2000hope}. Merit programs can
simultaneously increase total college-going among higher-income students
(who are more likely to meet eligibility thresholds) and improve the
composition of public institutions (because the public sector absorbs
disproportionately lower-income new entrants). The three-destination
sorting framework---public, private, and out-of-state---resolves what
has appeared to be a contradiction in the literature.

The cross-state evidence supports the model's comparative statics.
Massachusetts' Adams Scholarship, which shares Tennessee's design
features (moderate threshold, public-sector subsidy concentration),
independently produces a progressive shift of similar magnitude
($+1.38$ pp for bottom-quintile share). South Dakota's Opportunity
Scholarship, with a high ACT threshold and small award, produces a
strongly regressive shift---as the model predicts. West Virginia's
PROMISE Scholarship, despite similar design parameters to Tennessee,
does not produce progressive sorting because enrollment at WV publics
contracted during the analysis period. These results suggest that
program design determines the \emph{direction} of sorting, while
institutional context determines whether sorting \emph{operates}.

Four limitations merit emphasis. First, the analysis relies on
ecological inference from college-level aggregates. While the level
decomposition addresses the most direct concern, we cannot observe
individual enrollment decisions or track which students switch sectors.
Second, with 27 state-level clusters, inference is at the boundary of
reliable asymptotic approximation; we address this with permutation
inference. Third, the $\Delta_\text{SD}$ sensitivity analysis of
\textcite{rambachan2023more} indicates that results are fragile under
smoothness restrictions on differential trends. We interpret this as a
genuine bound on what the research design can deliver, not as evidence
against the effect. Fourth, the WV non-replication raises the question
of whether Tennessee's progressive result generalizes or reflects
state-specific conditions.

For policy, the findings suggest that the distributional consequences of
merit aid depend critically on program design. When eligibility
thresholds are set low enough that a substantial share of lower-income
students qualify, and when the subsidy is concentrated at public
institutions, merit aid can function as a progressive sorting mechanism.
The relevant counterfactual is not whether merit aid is more progressive
than need-based aid---it almost certainly is not---but whether merit aid
\emph{at the margin} improves or harms the composition of the
institutions that serve the most students. If states want merit programs
to improve access, the evidence suggests that the threshold should be
moderate (qualifying a broad share of the income distribution, not only
top students), the subsidy should be large enough to affect enrollment
decisions (not a token amount), and the institutional system should be
large enough to absorb compositional shifts without enrollment
contraction.

Descriptive evidence on upward mobility rates
(Section~\ref{sec:mobility}) suggests that the progressive composition
shift did not come at the cost of diminished returns: Tennessee public
colleges' mobility rates converged toward the national average in the
post-HOPE period.

Several directions for future research follow from these findings.
First, a structural model using individual-level administrative data
could identify the specific enrollment decisions---access expansion,
sector switching, out-of-state exit---that drive the aggregate
compositional shifts we document. Second, additional state cases with
large, stable public systems and clean program variation would test the
model's predictions more powerfully than the WV case, where enrollment
contraction confounds identification. Third, longer post-treatment
horizons would reveal whether the progressive shift persists or
attenuates as institutions adjust capacity and admissions standards.
Fourth, spatial spillover effects on neighboring states---if Tennessee
retains students who would otherwise cross borders---deserve
investigation as a potential general equilibrium channel.
