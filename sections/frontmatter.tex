\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Merit-based scholarship programs are often viewed as regressive
because eligibility correlates with family income. Using tax-linked
data from \textcite{chetty2020mobility}, we show that Tennessee's
HOPE Scholarship instead generates a \emph{progressive compositional
shift} at public colleges: the share of students from the bottom
income quintile rises while the top-quintile share falls. Total
enrollment remains unchanged, indicating that the effect operates
through sorting across institutional sectors rather than expansion
of college attendance.

We interpret this pattern through a three-destination sorting
model and decompose the effect into a permanent substitution
channel (the scholarship's price reduction) and a cyclical income
channel (sensitivity to local economic shocks). Merging county-level
income data, we show that tuition-scaling programs buffer these
income shocks, while fixed-dollar programs do not. Cross-state
evidence supports the model: programs with moderate eligibility
thresholds and large public-sector subsidies generate progressive
sorting, while high-threshold programs generate regressive
composition changes. Program design therefore determines both the
direction and durability of merit aid's distributional effects.
\end{abstract}
